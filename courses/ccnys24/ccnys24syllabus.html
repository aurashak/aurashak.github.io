    1. COURSE OVERVIEW

      This course presents theories and research methods for studying the recent history of global urbanization. Course material will cover the evolution of cities over time, including the reasons that cities have been established, how they have grown and maintained their populations, and why some have failed. Students will learn about the physical and social geographies of urban space, including the relationships between society and nature, various typologies of design and infrastructure, and the political spectrum of urban governance. This course will cover the process of urbanization from the street level, to the world systems of state politics, cultural ideologies, and multinational economics.

      This course seeks to answer the question: can cities be places of social and environmental justice, or will they continue on the path of social exclusion and environmental crisis?

    2. LEARNING OBJECTIVES

      During this course, students will learn about:

      • Major theories for why urbanization and de-urbanization have occurred in recent history
      • The impacts that urbanization patterns are having on contemporary local and global social inequality and environmental sustainability
      • State planning practices that intend to serve the common good
      • Community-based visions for urbanization that counter the state
      • Examples of compelling urban development projects, including parks, infrastructure, housing, transportation, and more
      • Methods for crafting theories of urban planning and design, from the local to global scale

    3. COURSE STRUCTURE + SCHEDULE

      This semester consists of four modules. Each module includes 2 readings per week and one assignment due at the end. Instructions for assignments will be posted on Blackboard.

      Module I: The Physical Geography of Cities (Weeks 1-3)

      This module explores urbanization as a global phenomenon. Students will study the range of spatial typologies that are exhibited by cities around the world. Students will learn how physical geographies, including proximity to natural resources, elevation levels, access to routes of transportation, and more, have contributed to the design of cities. This module also studies how physical geographies have shaped social practices in cities and vice versa, otherwise known as “urban political ecology”.

    Assignment #1: Spatial typology mapping

  • Complete the typologies of urban space exercise on Blackboard. In the exercise, you will identify multiple urban typologies including their core physical geographic elements, their physical parameters, proximity to natural resources, key pieces of infrastructure, and more. This assignment utilizes digital mapping techniques.

  • Module II: Uneven Development (Weeks 4-7)

    This module provides an overview of the power structures that have governed contemporary processes of urbanization. Like with other aspects of society, cities are outcomes of a political process. That political process is itself shaped by economics, culture, nature, and vice versa.

    By studying concepts of governance, political economy, and social justice, students will learn to determine the causes of inequality between large social groups in cities, a theoretical framework known as “uneven development”.

     

    Readings:

  • Week 4: Arrighi, G. (1994). The long twentieth century: Money, power, and the origins of our times. London: Verso. Pages 28 - 48.

  • Week 5 + 6: Parthasarathi, P. (2011). Why Europe Grew Rich and Asia Did Not: Global Economic Divergence, 1600–1850. Cambridge University Press.

  • Week 7: Bullard, R. and Chavis, B.F. Jr. (1993) Confronting Environmental Racism: Voices from the Grassroots. Boston, MA: South End Press. (Ch 1: Anatomy of Environmental Racism and the Environmental Justice Movement, pages 15-41).

     

    Assignment #2: Urban political profile

  • Complete the political anthropology exercise on Blackboard. In this exercise, you will learn the political structure of a city and how politics are reflected in the city's physical geography. Your research will show who political constituencies are, where they are located in the city, and how theories of governance translate into the socioeconomic conditions of communities in the city. This is a research and writing assignment.

  • Module III: Urbanization from below (Weeks 8-11)

    This module will cover the concept of urbanization from below, in which communities lead the process of research, planning, design, and management of urban space. Theories such as participatory planning, southern urbanism, municipalism, and others, will be used to explain the phenomenon in which communities protest state planning projects, while crafting their own vision for the future. Students will study these counter-planning efforts through the lens of urban social movements, such as environmentalism, the “Arab Spring”, the Paris Commune, and more.

     

    Readings:

  • Week 8: Harvey David. Rebel Cities: From the Right to the City to the Urban Revolution. Paperback ed. Verso 2013. Ch 3, p 67-88.

  • Week 9: Angotti, T. (2020). Transformative Planning: Radical Alternatives to Neoliberal Urbanism. Black Rose Books. (Ch 2: Resistance and Alternatives, pages 43-75).

  • Week 10: Akuno, K., & Nangwaya, A. (Eds.). (2017). Jackson Rising: The Struggle for Economic Democracy and Black Self-Determination in Jackson, Mississippi. Daraja Press.

  • Week 11: Khawarzad, Dawson, et al (2018). A Climate Action Plan for New York. CUNY Center for the Humanities.

     

    Assignment #3: Forensic analysis of urban protest

  • Conduct a forensic analysis of an urban protest action. Create a diagram of the public spaces that the protest was engaged in. Highlight aspects of the design that prohibited assembly and movement of protestors, such as surveillance technology, architectural elements, signage,

    and more. Document how state agencies, private organizations, and others, interacted with the protest action, either through policing it, enabling it, etc. Document your observations of how public space you studied works to facilitate public assemblies and protest, or discourage them.

  • Module IV: Worldbuilding (Weeks 12-15)

    Urbanization has happened so extensively that some have labeled our time the “urban age”.

    However, threats ranging from climate change, to political instability, to artificial intelligence, all possess the ability to increase urban social inequality, if not entirely destroy cities. This module presents futuristic visions being developed to avert or mitigate the impact of these threats.

    Students will review competing ideas for the future, ranging from techno-utopianism, to degrowth and preservation.

  • Readings:

  • Week 12: Robinson, Kim Stanley (2107). New York 2140. Orbit.

  • Week 13: Keith, V. and Studioteka (Firm) (2017) 2100: A Dystopian Utopia: The City After Climate Change. Terreform, Incorporated.

     

    Assignment #4: Worldbuilding

  • Communicate your vision for the future of the city. Create a map, drawing, model, or use another medium to show the social and physical geographies of your future city. Describe how your city will be successful given the threats to urban space the world will be experiencing.

    Describe/show the political economy of your city, the design of its built environment, how the infrastructure operates, and other key aspects of your urban space.

  • METHODOLOGIES

    This course is reading and design intensive. Each week will include assigned readings, along with supplemental materials in the form of podcasts, film clips, video lectures, and more. With the knowledge gained from the readings in each module, students will engage in design exercises, including cartography, graphic design, diagramming, archival documentation, and more. Some writing will be required for each assignment and for in-class exercises.

      1. GRADUATE STUDENT GRADING SYSTEM

      Course grades should reflect professional standards of ethics, timeliness, and participation,

      while also allowing room for intellectual and material experimentation by students. The “typical” grade for a course should be a B. Exceptional work can be recognized with an A or A-, but this

      work must exceed the expectation of the course and the assignments – and not merely meet those expectations in interesting ways. Brief descriptions of the letter grades are offered below as a

      guide to interpretation.

      1. The “A” grade should be given very rarely, and used to indication that a student has

        exceeded the expectations of the course to a very unusual degree. It should be used to indicate

        that a student has exceeded the expectations of the course in terms of comprehension, while also maintaining a high professional standard for ethical behavior, completeness and timeliness. The

      student’s demonstrated comprehension of the course material must be truly exceptional across each category of issues.


      A-

      The grade of “A-“ should also be given rarely. It should be used to indication that a student has exceeded the expectations of the course in terms of comprehension while also maintaining a high professional standard for ethical behavior, participation, completeness and timeliness.


      B+

      The grade of “B+” should be given to students who have completed their work in a

      professional and timely manner, and have exceeded the expectations of the course in some

      specific way that deserves special recognition – but have not exceeded expectations in all areas.


      1. The grade of “B” should be given to students who have completed their work in a professional manner, including demonstrating an ability to pursue questions and issues independently in their coursework.


      B-

      The grade of “B-“ should be given to students whose work or performance has been slightly less than fully professional in terms of completeness, timeliness or participation but shows

      promise in some other way.


      C+

      Students whose performances is incomplete or repeatedly late, or who are unprofessional

      in ethical terms, unwilling to cooperate with peers in group settings or whose work shows serious gaps in comprehension should receive grades lower than B-.

      F Students who clearly do not complete a course or whose work does not meet professional standards should receive a failing grade.


      INC Grades of “incomplete” are not given under any circumstances unless there is evidence

      of a medical or personal emergency. In such cases, instructor and student develop a contract to complete work by a specified date, as per CCNY policy. Classes and/or work missed due to illness must be explained with a physician’s note.


      For more information on grading guidelines and other CCNY policies and procedures, consult the current

      Academic Integrity

      Academic dishonesty is prohibited at the City University of New York and is punishable by

      penalties, including failing grades, suspension, and expulsion. Plagiarism (the presentation as one’s own work of words, drawings, ideas and opinions of someone else) is a serious instance of

      academic dishonesty. The submission of any piece of work (written, drawn, built, or photocopied) is assumed by the school to guarantee that the thoughts and expressions in it are literally the

      student’s own, executed by the student. All assignments must be the student’s original work,

      produced for this class and no other. Any copying, even short excerpts, from another book, article, or Internet source, published or unpublished, without proper attribution or citation will result in

      automatic failure of the entire course.


      You will fail this course if you: 1) submit work used for another course; 2) copy material and submit it as your own, without using quotations and citing your source, or in any other way represent the work of another person as your own; 3) submit the same work as another student. Plagiarized work will be reported to the CCNY Academic Integrity Committee, as per the CUNY Policy on Academic

      Integrity.


      CCNY Academic Integrity Policy: https://www.ccny.cuny.edu/about/integrity


      CLASSROOM EXPECTATIONS

      Classroom Civility

      All students are expected to participate in class discussions. Meaningful and constructive

      dialogue is encouraged; however, discussions should be civilized and respectful to everyone, as well as relevant to the topic being discussed.       All students are expected to engage in active learning during class time, including during remote Zoom instruction. The use of mobile phones, texting, chat features, and social media can be

      appropriate use of laptops will be discussed. The instructor will provide periodic breaks during in-person and Zoom classes; if needed, personal devices may be used during this time.
